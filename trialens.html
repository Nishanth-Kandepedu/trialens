About Drug Intelligence
Clinical SAR intelligence for drug discovery & medicinal chemistry teams
What is Drug Intelligence?

TriaLens is a clinical SAR intelligence platform for drug discovery and medicinal chemistry teams. Search any compound to instantly retrieve real clinical trial records from ClinicalTrials.gov, enriched with verified bioactivity data from ChEMBL and PubChem, and synthesised into structured SAR profiles with published literature citations.

Data Sources

ClinicalTrials.gov — NIH/FDA global trial registry. Trial metadata, phases, outcomes and descriptions are fetched live.

ChEMBL — EMBL-EBI bioactivity database. IC50, EC50, Ki, and binding assay values are retrieved directly.

PubChem — NCBI compound database. Molecular weight, LogP, TPSA, HBD/HBA, and rotatable bonds are fetched per compound.

Claude AI — Anthropic claude-opus-4-5. Used to synthesise and interpret the above real data, and to cite relevant published literature.

SAR Outputs

Potency & Efficacy — Real IC50/EC50/Ki values from ChEMBL, supplemented with published literature citations.

Dose-Response — AI-synthesised cohort response rates and toxicity grades based on clinical trial context.

ADMET — Physicochemical properties from PubChem (MW, LogP, TPSA) with AI-interpreted bioavailability, half-life, CYP, hERG, and BBB ratings citing published PK studies.

SAR Narrative — AI-generated pharmacology summary grounded in real database values and literature.

Disclaimer

Drug Intelligence combines real bioactivity data from ChEMBL and PubChem with AI-synthesised interpretation for research reference only. While potency values and physicochemical properties are sourced from peer-reviewed databases, ADMET predictions, dose-response summaries, and SAR narratives are AI-generated and may not reflect clinical reality. Always verify against primary literature before any research, commercial, or regulatory decision.

I'm Nishanth, a medicinal chemist working at the intersection of drug discovery data and SAR intelligence. Over the years, I've worked extensively with curated medicinal chemistry datasets and have seen firsthand how difficult it can be to connect clinical trial outcomes back to the molecules themselves.

Drug Intelligence started as a small experiment: what if you could type a compound name and instantly see the clinical trials, pharmacology, and SAR insights that matter?

This tool is my attempt to make clinical evidence more accessible and interpretable — helping medicinal and computational chemists connect molecules to their real-world clinical outcomes.
