About Drug Intelligence
Clinical SAR intelligence for drug discovery & medicinal chemistry teams
What is Drug Intelligence?

Drug Intelligence is a clinical SAR platform for medicinal chemists and drug hunters. Search any compound to instantly retrieve real clinical trial records from ClinicalTrials.gov, enriched with verified bioactivity data from ChEMBL and PubChem, and synthesised into structured SAR profiles with published literature citations.

Data Sources

ClinicalTrials.gov — NIH/FDA global trial registry. Trial metadata, phases, outcomes, interventions, eligibility criteria and posted results are fetched live.

ChEMBL — EMBL-EBI bioactivity database. IC50, EC50, Ki, Kd, and in vivo values are retrieved directly, classified into In Vitro and In Vivo tabs.

PubChem — NCBI compound database. Molecular structure, formula, MW, LogP, TPSA, HBD/HBA and rotatable bonds are fetched per compound.

Claude AI — Anthropic claude-opus-4-5. Used only to synthesise the SAR narrative from real database values. All tables are populated directly from ChEMBL and PubChem.

What You Get

Compound Structure — 2D structure image, molecular formula, IUPAC name and MW auto-loaded on every search from PubChem.

In Vitro Bioactivity — Real IC50/EC50/Ki/Kd values from ChEMBL, tabbed by endpoint type.

In Vivo Efficacy — Animal model PK and efficacy endpoints (AUC, Cmax, T½, ED50) from ChEMBL assay_type=F data.

ADMET — Absorption, Distribution, Metabolism, Excretion and Toxicity data from ChEMBL, rated good/moderate/poor.

SAR Narrative — AI-generated pharmacology summary grounded in real database values.

Trial Detail — Interventions & dosing, outcomes & measures, eligibility criteria, and posted results per NCT record.

Disclaimer

Drug Intelligence combines real bioactivity data from ChEMBL and PubChem with AI-synthesised interpretation for research reference only. While potency values and physicochemical properties are sourced from peer-reviewed databases, ADMET predictions, dose-response summaries, and SAR narratives are AI-generated and may not reflect clinical reality. Always verify against primary literature before any research, commercial, or regulatory decision.

Over the years, I've worked extensively with curated medicinal chemistry datasets and have seen firsthand how difficult it can be to connect clinical trial outcomes back to the molecules themselves.

Drug Intelligence started as a small experiment: what if you could type a compound name and instantly see the clinical trials, pharmacology, and SAR insights that matter?

This tool is my attempt to make clinical evidence more accessible and interpretable — helping medicinal and computational chemists connect molecules to their real-world clinical outcomes.
